## Archaeological Discovery

### Knossos Ivory Scepter — Longest Linear A Inscription (2025)

**What was found:** An ivory scepter (ring + handle) bearing the longest known Linear A inscription — approximately **119 signs** — discovered in the Anetaki plot of Knossos, within an identified Cult Center.

**Context:** The object was found in a semi-subterranean structure called the "Ivory Deposit" within a Neopalatial-phase building (1700–1450 BCE). The deposit also contained pottery, bronze, and offering-related items, confirming a clear religious context.

**Description of the object:**
- Two components: an ivory ring (carved from a single elephant tusk) and a handle
- The ring has two bored holes, likely for suspending a perishable object (rope, cord, organic material)
- Epigraphic analysis found **stylistic differences** between the ring and handle inscriptions, suggesting different scribes or different engraving times

**The inscription:**
- ~119 symbols total — mixture of logograms and syllabic characters
- Ring face: twelve quadrupeds each within a metope — probable ritual inventory of animals for sacrifice
- Other faces: syllabic + logographic signs associated with vessel signs (possibly noting contents)
- **No numerals** on the ritual section — distinguishes this from administrative tablets
- Handle: administrative-style inscription including numerical and fraction signs → **dual-use object** (ritual + accounting)

**Publication:** Kanta, A., Nakassis, D., Palaima, T. G., & Perna, M. (2025). "An archaeological and epigraphical overview of some inscriptions found in the Cult Center of the city of Knossos (Anetaki plot)." *Ariadne*, 27–43. doi: 10.26248/ariadne.vi.1841

**Relevance to this project:**
- Strongest external confirmation yet that Linear A had a robust, structurally distinct non-administrative register — directly supports our ritual register findings (IOZa2, libation formula analysis)
- The dual-use nature (ritual ring + administrative handle by different scribes) mirrors the dual register we observe across the corpus
- The logogram inventory (animals, vessels, textiles, hides) in ritual context may help identify our unknown ritual logograms
- Adds 119 signs to the global corpus; at ~7,362 total signs this is a meaningful ~1.6% increase

## Scholarly Publications

### Salgarella & Petrakis (eds.) 2026 — *The Wor(l)ds of Linear A*

**Full title:** *The Wor(l)ds of Linear A: Interdisciplinary Approaches to Documents and Inscriptions of a Cretan Bronze Age Script*

**Published:** January 2026, *Athens University Review of Archaeology (AURA)*, ISSN 2623-3428. Open access: https://epub.lib.uoa.gr/index.php/aura/article/view/2642

**Background:** This volume collects papers from the first international meeting devoted entirely to Linear A. It covers both specialized studies and macroscopic surveys of the evidence, representing the current state of the field.

**Selected contributing papers:**
- **B. Davis** — "Recent linguistics-based advances in the study of Linear A" *(see Davis entry below)*
- **C. M. Donnelly** — "Cycladic Linear A and Connections between Scripts" — examines non-Cretan occurrences of Linear A and sign relationships across Aegean scripts; relevant to our cross-site analysis
- **I. Schoep** — "The impact of the Minoan chronological framework on the study of Minoan and Mycenaean writing and sealing practices" — temporal context for the administrative corpus

**Relevance to this project:** Davis's chapter directly addresses current methodological advances using approaches similar to ours (positional analysis, structural linguistics). Donnelly's chapter may clarify the geographic distribution of our ritual formulas (which appear at Cycladic sites). The volume represents the most current scholarly consensus in the field.

**Sources:**
- [AWOL — The Ancient World Online: Wor(l)ds of Linear A](http://ancientworldonline.blogspot.com/2026/02/the-worlds-of-linear-interdisciplinary.html)

### Nepal & Perono Cacciafoco (2024) — Minoan Cryptanalysis

**Full title:** "Minoan Cryptanalysis: Computational Approaches to Deciphering Linear A and Assessing Its Connections with Language Families from the Mediterranean and the Black Sea Areas"

**Published:** MDPI *Information*, Vol. 15, No. 2, Article 73. January 25, 2024. doi: 10.3390/info15020073. URL: https://www.mdpi.com/2078-2489/15/2/73

**Authors:** Aaradh Nepal (Xi'an Jiaotong-Liverpool Univ.) and Francesco Perono Cacciafoco (Xi'an Jiaotong-Liverpool Univ.)

**Method:**
1. Feature-based similarity measure comparing Linear A signs to the Carian Alphabet and Cypriot Syllabary — produces new phonetic value hypotheses for some signs
2. Consonantal comparison (removing vowels) of derived Linear A readings against Ancient Egyptian, Luwian, Hittite, Proto-Celtic, and Uralic using a Python program

**Key findings:**
- Found vocabulary-level matches with Luwian ("PA-RE" ↔ Luwian *pari* = "forth/away")
- Comparisons to Hittite also yielded some matches
- Egyptian, Proto-Celtic, and Uralic: limited matches
- Only 43 phonetic value matches derived — the study is limited by the small phonetic grid from the feature-based step

**Limitations acknowledged by the authors:**
- Consonantal approach loses vowel information
- Matches could represent lexical borrowings rather than genetic relationship
- The small derived phonetic grid limits the scope of language comparisons

**Relevance to this project:**
- External validation that Luwian vocabulary-level matches exist — consistent with our 0 high-confidence *morpheme* alignment result; vocabulary matches ≠ genetic relationship
- Their consonantal/cluster approach is conceptually similar to our n-gram fingerprinting strategy (Strategy 9 in CLAUDE.md)
- "PA-RE" ↔ Luwian *pari* is a notable specific match — worth checking if PA-RE appears in our corpus in a position consistent with an adverb/particle meaning "forth/away"

**Sources:**
- [MDPI paper](https://www.mdpi.com/2078-2489/15/2/73)
- [Academia.edu PDF](https://www.academia.edu/122199164/Minoan_Cryptanalysis_Computational_Approaches_to_Deciphering_Linear_A_and_Assessing_Its_Connections_with_Language_Families_from_the_Mediterranean_and_the_Black_Sea_Areas_In_Computational_Linguistics_and_Natural_Language_Processing_)

### "Minoan and the Machines" (2024–2025) — Review Paper

**Full title:** "Minoan and the Machines: Computational Approaches to the Decipherment of Linear A"

**Published:** *Annals of the University of Craiova, Series Philology — Linguistics*. URL: https://anale-lingvistica.reviste.ucv.ro/index.php/laucv/article/view/134

**Content:** Review of significant literature on computational Linear A decipherment. Summarizes:
- Minimum cost-flow model for script decipherment — achieved **67.3% accuracy** on Linear B as a validation test; applied to Linear A with limited success
- Generative framework for phonetic value assignment
- Consonantal cluster-based brute-force cryptanalysis (Perono Cacciafoco lineage)
- Feature-based sign similarity approach

**Relevance to this project:** The 67.3% accuracy figure on Linear B (the deciphered comparison) sets an upper bound for what current ML methods can achieve without a known language. Our positional/distributional analysis does not rely on phonetic value assignments, which may give it more robustness.

**Sources:**
- [Annals of the University of Craiova](https://anale-lingvistica.reviste.ucv.ro/index.php/laucv/article/view/134)
- [Academia.edu PDF](https://www.academia.edu/127802145/Minoan_and_the_Machines_Computational_Approaches_to_the_Decipherment_of_Linear_A)

### Brent Davis — VSO Word Order and Libation Formula Analysis

**Researcher:** Dr. Brent Davis, Lecturer in Archaeology and Ancient Egyptian, Univ. of Melbourne

**Key finding:** Established **Verb-Subject-Object (VSO) word order** for Minoan — the first demonstration of Minoan word order from corpus evidence.

**Method:**
- Analyzed the "Libation Formula" — a recurring sequence on Minoan ritual stone vessels (the same dataset as our IOZa2 and ritual register analysis)
- Observed that the second word in the formula always varies across bowls while other positions remain fixed
- Inferred the varying element is the subject (personal name of the dedicant)
- Confirmed by finding the Linear B olive sign (an object) following the name in the sequence
- Eliminated SOV (like early Greek/Sumerian) in favor of VSO (like ancient Egyptian)

**Broader characterization:** Non-Indo-European, non-Semitic, verb-initial agglutinative language.

**Chapter:** B. Davis, "Recent linguistics-based advances in the study of Linear A," in Salgarella & Petrakis (eds.) 2026.

**Relevance to this project:**
- Davis is analyzing the **exact same ritual inscriptions** we are working with (IOZa2, libation tables, stone vessels)
- VSO order means our slot assignments in the IOZa2 formula should be re-examined: if position 1 is the verb, what is A-TA-I-*301-WA-JA? A ritual verb ("I offer/dedicate")?
- This could reframe our entire IOZa2 template: `[VERB phrase] [SUBJECT — dedicant name / DI-KI] [OBJECTS — JA-SA-SA-RA-ME, U-NA-KA-NA-SI, ...]`
- Caution: VSO established for the Libation Formula; IOZa2 is a stone vessel inscription but more complex — verify assumption applies

**Sources:**
- [How Do You Crack the Code to a Lost Ancient Script? — Univ. of Melbourne](https://pursuit.unimelb.edu.au/articles/how-do-you-crack-the-code-to-a-lost-ancient-script/)

## Databases and Digital Infrastructure

### SigLA — The Signs of Linear A: A Palæographical Database

**URL:** https://sigla.phis.me

**Created by:** Dr. Ester Salgarella (Univ. of Cambridge) and Dr. Simon Castellan (INRIA, Univ. of Rennes). Launched 2020.

**Coverage:**
- 3,000+ individual signs drawn from 400 inscriptions
- All known Linear A sites
- 300 standard symbols in the sign list
- Each sign entry shows actual drawn occurrences (not standardized abstract shapes)

**Features:**
- Cross-search by sign, sign-sequence (word), position within inscription, frequency
- Paleographic variation: intra-site (scribe-to-scribe) and inter-site (site-to-site) comparisons
- Advanced filtered searches by site, support type, and other parameters

**Publication:** Salgarella, E. & Castellan, S. (2021). "The Signs of Linear A: a palæographical database." In *Grapholinguistics in the 21st Century 2020*, Fluxus Editions, Brest, pp. 945–962. doi: 10.36824/2020-graf-salg

**Relevance to this project:**
- Cross-referencing unknown signs (especially *301, *22F, *304, *308) against SigLA's paleographic data may clarify their shape relationships to known signs
- Inter-site paleographic variation data complements our scribe-field analysis
- Our corpus (linear_a_llm_master.jsonl) has 1,721 records vs. SigLA's ~400 inscriptions — our corpus is larger in record count; SigLA provides sign-level shape data we lack
- Sign-sequence position data in SigLA could cross-validate our positional analysis findings

**Sources:**

## Caution: Low-Quality Claims to Disregard

Several recent Academia.edu papers claim full or partial decipherment of Linear A. These should be treated with extreme caution:

- **"The Decipherment of Linear A: A Breakthrough in Minoan Linguistics"** (Academia.edu, 2025) — Claims 100+ deciphered inscriptions. Not peer-reviewed. Uses assumptions not grounded in systematic corpus analysis. Do not incorporate claims without independent verification.
- **"Deciphering Linear A: A Structured Minoan Script for Trade and Religious Administration"** (Academia.edu, 2025) — Claims 1,500 inscriptions analyzed with statistical frequency; refutes Indo-European and claims structured translations. Not peer-reviewed. Statistical claims are unverifiable without methodology.
- **"Linear A is deciphered"** (Academia.edu) — Title speaks for itself. Not substantiated.

These papers appear regularly in search results but do not represent scholarly consensus and should not be cited as evidence in hypotheses.
